U.S. SENATE

Established by the constitution with the purpose of acting as one part of the Legislative Branch of the federal government, the United States Senate along with the House of Representatives are fundamental in the functionality of the United States of America. The Senate is made up of one hundred Senators (two from each state) who serve six year terms. These terms, however, begin and end at different times, so that one third of senators are up for election every two years.

Senators (along with members of the House of Representatives) are required to propose, debate, write and then vote on legislation which can range from social security to foreign relations with another nation. In order for a bill to be passed, it is usually required to pass a vote in both the House, Senate and then be signed by the President of the United States before it is enacted into law. The Senate also has a few unique jobs which include agreeing to treaties with foreign nations and confirming Presidential appointments such as judges, foreign ambassadors, and cabinet positions.
